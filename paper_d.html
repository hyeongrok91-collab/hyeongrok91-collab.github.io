Consensus-Target Linear Shrinkage: The Geometry of In-Context Learning in the Gradient-Gram Space

Hyeong Rok Lee · March 2026 · Preprint
📄 PDF 🔗 DOI

Abstract

In-context learning (ICL) is widely observed to improve language model performance by providing task examples in the prompt, yet a precise mathematical characterization of what ICL does to the model's internal geometry remains open. Under a shared-component decomposition, ICL performs consensus-target linear shrinkage on the task-gradient Gram matrix:

$$G^P = \alpha_P\, J + (1-\alpha_P)\,G^r, \qquad \alpha_P = \frac{\|a_P\|^2}{\|a_P\|^2 + r^2},$$

where $G^P$ and $G^r$ are the gradient-cosine Gram matrices with and without prefix, $J$ is the all-ones consensus target, and $\alpha_P \in [0,1]$ is the adaptation parameter. This identity implies an Orthogonal Contraction Law: the non-consensus geometry contracts by exactly $(1-\alpha_P)$, and an Effective-Rank Bound: $\mathrm{erank}(G^P) \le \exp(h(\alpha_P))\cdot(n-1)^{1-\alpha_P}$.

A 65-point sweep across 5 tasks confirms that compression ratio is a near-deterministic function of $\alpha_P$ (per-task $R^2 > 0.98$), establishing $\alpha_P$ as the master variable of ICL. Cross-model validation on 8 architectures (GPT-2 to Phi-2, 117M–2.7B parameters, 2019–2025) shows prediction error below 3% in all cases.

Combined with the intelligence decomposition of Paper B, $\alpha_P$ governs the compression law ($\Delta\tau_{\mathrm{rep}}$), while the task-dependent quality factor $Q$ governs the variable reward ($\Delta Q$). Together they yield the Adaptation Cone Law on constant-ECC leaves, constraining intelligence-improving adaptations to a geometric cone in the $(\Delta\log\tau_{\mathrm{rep}},\, \Delta\log Q)$ plane.

1. Introduction

1.1 Context and motivation

In-context learning—the ability of large language models to improve on a task when provided with input–output examples in the prompt—is one of the most striking emergent capabilities of modern AI. Despite extensive study, the field lacks a quantitative law describing what ICL does to the model's internal computational geometry.

This paper proposes such a law. We work in the gradient-Gram space: for a set of $n$ task inputs, we compute per-input gradients of the loss and form their cosine-similarity Gram matrix. This object encodes how "aligned" the model's responses are across different inputs.

1.3 A counter-intuitive discovery

ICL compresses the gradient geometry. The effective rank drops from 2.09 to 1.25 (arithmetic task, Qwen3-0.6B). Yet intelligence increases by 201%.

The resolution is that compression is not information loss; it is consensus formation. The prefix aligns gradients toward a shared task direction, collapsing diversity in favor of coherence. The quality factor $Q$ (from Paper B) then over-compensates the representational loss.

2. Consensus-Target Shrinkage Theorem

2.1 Setup and notation

Fix a pretrained language model with parameters $\theta$ and a target parameter module. Given $n$ task inputs $\{x_1,\ldots,x_n\}$, define:

  • $u_i^r = \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(x_i) / \|\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(x_i)\|_2$: the $\ell_2$-normalized gradient without prefix ("raw").
  • $u_i^P = \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\mathrm{prefix} \circ x_i) / \|\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\mathrm{prefix} \circ x_i)\|_2$: the $\ell_2$-normalized gradient with prefix.

The gradient-Gram matrices are:

$$G^r_{ij} = \langle u_i^r, u_j^r \rangle, \qquad G^P_{ij} = \langle u_i^P, u_j^P \rangle.$$

Both are $n \times n$ positive semidefinite matrices with unit diagonal.

Definition (Consensus Target). $J \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is the all-ones matrix: $J_{ij} = 1$ for all $i,j$. This represents perfect consensus—all gradients perfectly aligned.

2.2 Conditional Gram decomposition

Theorem (Consensus-Target Linear Shrinkage). Suppose each prefix gradient decomposes as $$u_i^P = a_P + r_i, \qquad a_P \perp r_i, \qquad \|r_i\| = r \quad \text{(constant)},$$ where $a_P$ is the shared consensus component and $r_i$ is the residual, and with the identification $G^r_{ij} = \langle r_i, r_j \rangle / r^2$. For $r > 0$: $$\boxed{G^P = \alpha_P\, J + (1-\alpha_P)\,G^r, \qquad \alpha_P = \frac{\|a_P\|^2}{\|a_P\|^2 + r^2}.}$$ When $r = 0$, every $u_i^P = a_P$ is identical, $\alpha_P = 1$, and $G^P = J$.
Proof. Since $\|u_i^P\|^2 = \|a_P\|^2 + r^2 =: s^2$ by orthogonality: $$G^P_{ij} = \langle u_i^P, u_j^P \rangle = \frac{1}{s^2}\big(\|a_P\|^2 + \langle r_i, r_j \rangle\big) = \frac{\|a_P\|^2}{s^2} + \frac{r^2}{s^2} \cdot \frac{\langle r_i, r_j \rangle}{r^2} = \alpha_P \cdot 1 + (1-\alpha_P) \cdot G^r_{ij}.$$ In matrix form, $G^P = \alpha_P J + (1-\alpha_P)G^r$. ∎
Remark (Quality of the orthogonality assumption). The assumption $a_P \perp r_i$ holds approximately. Direct measurement gives:

2.3 Orthogonal Contraction Law

Theorem (Orthogonal Contraction). Let $u = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\mathbf{1}$ and $P_\perp = I - uu^\top$. Then: $$\boxed{P_\perp G^P P_\perp = (1-\alpha_P) \cdot P_\perp G^r P_\perp.}$$
Proof. Since $P_\perp J P_\perp = P_\perp(n\,uu^\top)P_\perp = n(P_\perp u)(u^\top P_\perp) = 0$: $$P_\perp G^P P_\perp = P_\perp\big[\alpha_P J + (1-\alpha_P)G^r\big]P_\perp = \alpha_P \cdot 0 + (1-\alpha_P) P_\perp G^r P_\perp = (1-\alpha_P) P_\perp G^r P_\perp. \qquad \square$$
Remark (Physical Interpretation). The consensus direction $\mathbf{1}/\sqrt{n}$ absorbs the "shared signal." Everything orthogonal to it—the entire non-consensus geometry—contracts by the factor $(1-\alpha_P)$. At $\alpha_P = 0.966$, only 3.4% of the original structure survives.

2.4 Effective-Rank Entropy Bound

Corollary (Entropy Bound on Effective Rank). Under the cumulative-overlap condition $\eta_k \ge s_k$ for all $k < n$, the Ky Fan variational principle implies: $$\boxed{\mathrm{erank}(G^P) \le \exp(h(a)) \cdot (n-1)^{1-a},}$$ where $a=p_1^P=\lambda_1(G^P)/n$ and $h(a)=-a\log a - (1-a)\log(1-a)$ is the binary entropy.

3.4 The D×B resolution

$$\Delta \log I = \underbrace{\Delta \log \tau_{\mathrm{rep}}}_{\text{D explains (universal)}} + \underbrace{\Delta \log Q}_{\text{B explains (task-dependent)}}.$$

Paper D provides the compression law: $\Delta\tau_{\mathrm{rep}} = g(\alpha_P)$ with $R^2 > 0.98$. Paper B provides the task-dependent quality law: $\Delta Q$ varies with task structure. Together they account for $\Delta I$.

4. Adaptation Cone Law

4.1 Constant-ECC leaves

Empirically, ECC is invariant under both ICL and fine-tuning to machine precision ($\mathrm{CV} < 10^{-4}$). On a constant-ECC leaf, the state is described by two coordinates:

$$x := \Delta\log\tau_{\mathrm{rep}}, \qquad q := \Delta\log Q.$$

4.2 Compressive-expansive dichotomy

Theorem (Adaptation Cone). The intelligence-improving region $\mathcal{H} = \{(x,q) : x + q > 0\}$ decomposes as: $$\mathcal{H} = \mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{comp}} \sqcup \mathcal{I} \sqcup \mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{exp}},$$
Sector Condition Interpretation
$\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{comp}}$ $x < 0$, $q> |x|$ compression + mandatory $Q$ over-compensation
$\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{exp}}$ $x > 0$, $x+q > 0$ expansion + $Q$ compensation optional
$\mathcal{I}$ $x = 0$, $q > 0$ interface (neutral line)
Remark (ICL and FT occupy opposite cones).
  • ICL: $\dot x \le 0$, $\dot x + \dot q \ge 0$ (compressive, reversible, requires $Q$ compensation).
  • FT: $\dot x \ge 0$, $\dot x + \dot q \ge 0$ (expansive, irreversible, $Q$ sign undetermined).

4.3 Compensation necessity

Theorem (Compensation Necessity). In an ECC-preserving adaptation with $\Delta\log\tau_{\mathrm{rep}} < 0$ and $\Delta\log I> 0$: $$\boxed{\Delta\log Q > |\Delta\log\tau_{\mathrm{rep}}|.}$$

This is the analog of Carnot's constraint: compressive improvement requires quality over-compensation.

4.4 Synergy bound

Theorem (Interaction Decomposition). For a composed adaptation $F$ consisting of compression $C$ followed by expansion $E$: $$\Delta_F \log I = \Delta_C \log I + \Delta_E \log I + \Xi,$$ where $\Xi = \frac{1}{2} st\,[X_C, X_E](\log I) + O(\|(s,t)\|^3)$, so $\Xi \neq 0$ whenever the commutator acts nontrivially on $\log I$.

Empirically, ICL followed by FT gives different results than FT followed by ICL (overlap $= 0.503$), confirming nonzero $\Xi$ in practice.

7. Discussion

Why every theory opens exactly one next question

Newton's $F = ma$ opened "What is $F$?" Shannon's $C = B\log(1+\text{SNR})$ opened "What code achieves $C$?" Paper D answers "What does ICL do in gradient-Gram space?" and opens "What determines $\alpha_P$?"—the subject of Paper E.

The limit of a single variable

Within a single task, $\mathrm{corr}(\alpha_P, \Delta I) = 0.98$. Across 5 tasks, the correlation drops to $R^2 = 0.39$. This is not a failure but a structural fact: $\alpha_P$ governs $\Delta\tau_{\mathrm{rep}}$, but $\Delta I$ additionally depends on $\Delta Q$.

8. Conclusion

Paper A substituted computation for geometry. Paper B substituted performance for decomposition. Paper D substitutes adaptation for consensus shrinkage. All three follow the same pattern: replace a known computation with the right mathematics, and counter-intuitive structure appears.

ICL compresses the gradient geometry toward a consensus mode. The Orthogonal Contraction Theorem shows this compression is exactly $(1-\alpha_P)$, and the Entropy Bound yields an effective-rank upper bound numerically close to observed values ($\approx 1.24$ vs. $\approx 1.25$ in the arithmetic task). Cross-model validation on 8 architectures confirms the shrinkage law with prediction error below 3% in all cases.

The discovery that $\alpha_P$ is the master variable of ICL parallels Carnot's identification of $T_c/T_h$ as the efficiency bound: a single measurable quantity determines the ceiling. Combined with Paper B's quality factor $Q$, the complete picture is the Adaptation Cone Law: on constant-ECC leaves, ICL occupies the compressive cone while fine-tuning occupies the expansive cone.

What determines $\alpha_P$—the "Boltzmann question" of this framework—is the subject of Paper E.
