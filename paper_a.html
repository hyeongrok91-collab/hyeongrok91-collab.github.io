The d² Pullback Theorem: Why Attention is a d²-Dimensional Problem

Hyeong Rok Lee · March 2026 · Preprint
📄 PDF 🔗 DOI

Abstract

Self-attention is usually described as an $n \times n$ operation because it materializes all token pairs. We argue that this is the wrong intrinsic dimension. For a single head with token matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$ and parameter product $M = W_Q W_K^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$, the score matrix has the constrained bilinear form

$$S(M) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{d}} X M X^\top.$$

Our first result is the d² pullback theorem: for any differentiable loss through a row-wise attention map, the gradient with respect to $M$ is exactly the sandwich transform $X^\top G(M) X / \sqrt{d}$, where $G(M)$ is the gradient in token-pair space. Hence optimization depends only on the bilinear score subspace $\mathcal{S}_X = \{XAX^\top : A \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}\}$, whose dimension is $\mathrm{rank}(X)^2$ and equals $d^2$ whenever $X$ has full column rank.

Our second result computes the exact local Jacobian rank for analytic row normalizers:

$$\mathrm{rank}\, D_M P(0) = r(r - \varepsilon_X),$$

where $r = \mathrm{rank}(X)$ and $\varepsilon_X = \dim(\mathrm{col}(X) \cap \mathrm{span}\{\mathbf{1}_n\})$. Thus the generic rank is $d^2$ in the standard full-rank regime.

Our third result proves same-depth uniform replacement of softmax attention by positive polynomial attention on compact bounded-score domains. We then formulate a simulation hypothesis under which each degree-$p$ polynomial block can be replaced by $1 + O(\log p)$ degree-2 residual attention sublayers, and prove a no-go theorem: universal fixed-width degree-2 simulation requires $L(2w^2+3) \ge p$.

1. Introduction

Attention began as a mechanism for alignment in neural machine translation. The Transformer converted that alignment mechanism into the central computational primitive of modern sequence models. Since then, a large literature has tried to reduce the quadratic cost of forming attention matrices. The dominant question is computational: how can one approximate or avoid the $n \times n$ attention matrix without sacrificing accuracy?

This paper asks a different question. What is the intrinsic dimension of the attention optimization problem? For a single head, the pre-softmax scores are generated by a bilinear form in $d$ dimensions:

$$S(M) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{d}} X M X^\top.$$

Thus the pre-softmax score geometry is controlled by at most $d^2$ degrees of freedom, not $n^2$.

The crucial observation is that optimization never sees the full token-pair gradient directly. By the chain rule, the gradient with respect to $M$ is obtained by sandwiching the token-pair gradient between $X^\top$ and $X$. This induces a precise pullback from the $n^2$-dimensional token-pair world to a $d^2$-dimensional parameter world.

Main contributions

  1. The d² pullback theorem: the exact gradient w.r.t. $M$ is the pullback $X^\top G(M) X / \sqrt{d}$, so optimization depends only on a subspace of dimension $\mathrm{rank}(X)^2$.
  2. Exact local Jacobian rank for analytic row normalizers: $r(r - \varepsilon_X)$, generically $d^2$.
  3. Same-depth positive polynomial replacement of softmax on compact bounded-score domains (unconditional).
  4. Conditional depth compensation: degree-2 attention emulates degree-$p$ polynomial attention with $L + O(L \log p)$ total sublayers under a simulation hypothesis.
  5. No-go theorem: universal fixed-width simulation requires $L(2w^2+3) \ge p$.

2. Attention as Euclidean Matching

The pre-softmax score matrix $C = QK^\top$ factors through a $d$-dimensional latent space, so $\mathrm{rank}(C) \le d$. Dot-product matching is equivalent to Euclidean bipartite matching up to an additive constant:

Proposition (Attention as Euclidean matching). $$\max_{\pi \in S_n} \sum_{i=1}^n q_i^\top k_{\pi(i)} = \frac{1}{2}\sum_i \|q_i\|^2 + \frac{1}{2}\sum_j \|k_j\|^2 - \frac{1}{2}\min_{\pi} \sum_i \|q_i - k_{\pi(i)}\|^2.$$

Thus the pre-softmax attention scores encode a Euclidean assignment geometry in $\mathbb{R}^d$. Softmax turns these scores into row-stochastic weights, but that transformation is rank-inflating, obscuring the underlying bilinear geometry.

3. The Master Pullback Theorem

Theorem (Master pullback theorem). For each attention head $h$ with query tokens $X_h$, key tokens $Y_h$, score matrix $S_h(M_h) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{d_h}} X_h M_h Y_h^\top + B_h$, and loss $\mathcal{J}$: $$\boxed{\nabla_{M_h} \mathcal{J} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{d_h}} X_h^\top G_h(M_h) Y_h.}$$ The gradient lies in the bilinear pullback subspace $\mathcal{S}_{X_h,Y_h} := \{X_h^\top A Y_h : A \in \mathbb{R}^{n_q \times n_k}\}$, with $\dim \mathcal{S}_{X_h,Y_h} = \mathrm{rank}(X_h) \cdot \mathrm{rank}(Y_h)$.
Proof. By the chain rule, $D\mathcal{J}(M_h)[\Delta] = \langle G_h, \frac{1}{\sqrt{d_h}} X_h \Delta Y_h^\top \rangle_F = \langle \frac{1}{\sqrt{d_h}} X_h^\top G_h Y_h, \Delta \rangle_F$ using the Frobenius adjoint identity. For the dimension, rank factorizations $X_h = U_q R_q$ and $Y_h = U_k R_k$ give $\dim \mathcal{S} = r_q \cdot r_k$. ∎
Remark (Optimization only sees the pullback). If two token-pair gradients $G_1, G_2$ differ by an element orthogonal to $\mathcal{S}_{X_h,Y_h}$, they induce the same parameter gradient. Thus the optimization-relevant object is the pullback, not the full $n^2$-dimensional field.
Remark (Cross-attention is lower-dimensional). If the encoder output has effective rank $r_k < d_h$, then $\dim \mathcal{S}=r_q \cdot r_k < d_h^2$. This explains why cross-attention often converges faster.
Corollary (Second-order pullback). If $\mathcal{J}$ is $C^2$ in the score matrix, then the Hessian of $\mathcal{J}$ with respect to $M_h$ also respects the pullback subspace.

4. The Generic Rank Theorem

4.1 Analytic row normalizers

Theorem (Exact local rank formula). Let $P(M) = \Sigma(S(M))$ with analytic permutation-equivariant row normalizer. Define $r := \mathrm{rank}(X)$ and $\varepsilon_X := \dim(\mathrm{col}(X) \cap \mathrm{span}\{\mathbf{1}_n\})$. Then: $$\boxed{\mathrm{rank}\, D_M P(0) = r(r - \varepsilon_X).}$$ In the standard full-rank regime ($\mathrm{rank}(X) = d$, $\mathbf{1}_n \notin \mathrm{col}(X)$), this gives $\mathrm{rank}\, D_M P(0) = d^2$.
Proof sketch. The derivative $Ds(0) = \alpha \Pi_n$ (the centering projection) by permutation-equivariance and row-stochasticity. Vectorizing gives $\mathrm{rank}\, D_M P(0) = \mathrm{rank}(\Pi_n X) \cdot \mathrm{rank}(X) = (r - \varepsilon_X) \cdot r$. Analyticity + nonzero minor at origin → full-rank set is open and dense. ∎

4.2 Noncritical extension (covering $x^2$)

Theorem (Noncritical generic rank). For an analytic kernel $\phi$ with $\phi(x)\phi'(x) \neq 0$ on a noncritical interval, define the row kernel direction $\kappa(z) := \phi(z) \oslash \phi'(z)$ and the kernel bundle $\mathcal{K}_Z$. If $\mathcal{S}_X \cap \mathcal{K}_Z = \{0\}$ at a witness point $M_*$, then $\mathrm{rank}\, D_M P(M_*) = d^2$, and the full-rank set is open and dense.

4.3 Master Jacobian-rank theorem (unifying)

Theorem (Master Jacobian-rank theorem). For any $P(M) = \Sigma(S(M))$ with $S(M) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{d}} XMX^\top + B$: $$\boxed{\mathrm{rank}\, D_M P(M_*) = r^2 - \dim(\mathcal{S}_X \cap \ker D\Sigma(Z_*)).}$$ This covers masked/causal attention with visible sets $J_i$ by replacing $\ker D\Sigma$ with the masked kernel bundle $\mathcal{K}_Z^J$.

4.4 FFN Jacobian-span bound

Theorem (FFN Jacobian-span bound). For a residual FFN $F(x) = x + W_2 \sigma(W_1 x + b_1) + b_2$: $$\mathrm{vec}(J_F(x) - I_d) = (W_1^\top \odot W_2) \sigma'(W_1 x + b_1),$$ $$\dim \mathcal{J}_{\mathrm{FFN}} \le \mathrm{rank}(W_1^\top \odot W_2) \le \min(m, d^2).$$

5. Same-Depth Positive Polynomial Replacement

Theorem (Same-depth positive polynomial replacement). Let $T = B_{\exp,L} \circ \cdots \circ B_{\exp,1}$ be a residual softmax transformer stack on a compact set $\mathcal{K}$ with bounded scores $|Z_{ij}| \le R$. Then for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists a positive polynomial $P$ (constructed via squaring: $P(x) = R_q(x)^2 + \delta^2$) such that the same-depth stack $\widetilde{T}$ using $P$ in place of $\exp$ satisfies: $$\sup_{X \in \mathcal{K}} \|T(X) - \widetilde{T}(X)\|_\infty \le \varepsilon.$$

The proof combines Weierstrass approximation (for scalar $\exp \to P$), normalization stability (Lemma), and a telescoping hybrid argument across layers.

6. Conditional Depth Compensation for Degree-2 Attention

Residual quadratic degree growth

If each residual sublayer has degree $\le 2$, then $m$ composed sublayers produce degree $\le 2^m$ — so $m = O(\log p)$ sublayers suffice to reach degree $p$ in principle.

Hypothesis (Stable simulation hypothesis). Fix a compact set $\mathcal{K}$ and a degree-$p$ positive polynomial attention block $B_P : \mathcal{K} \to \mathcal{K}$. There exist constants $c_0, c_1 > 0$ and a residual stack $\widehat{B}_{2,p,\zeta} = G_m \circ \cdots \circ G_1$ with $m \le c_0 + c_1 \log p$ quadratic sublayers that uniformly approximates $B_P$ to accuracy $\zeta$ on $\mathcal{K}$ while preserving $\mathcal{K}$-invariance and Lipschitz control.
Theorem (Conditional depth compensation). Under the simulation hypothesis, each softmax block can be replaced by $1 + O(\log p)$ quadratic residual sublayers, giving total depth $L + O(L \log p)$.

7. No-Go Theorem for Universal Degree-2 Simulation

Theorem (No-go for universal degree-2 simulation). Fix a canonical degree-2 stack with fixed auxiliary width $w$ and $L$ layers (total parameters $N = L(2w^2+3)$). If this stack can uniformly approximate a degree-$p$ anchored positive polynomial target family to arbitrary precision on test inputs, then: $$\boxed{N \ge p.}$$
Proof idea. The target family has $p$ free parameters controlling distinct anchored polynomial coefficients. A Vandermonde argument shows these parameters trace out a $p$-dimensional image. The degree-2 stack maps are rational in their $N$ parameters, so their image is semialgebraic with dimension $\le N$. If the stack image is dense in the target image, then $N \ge p$ by semialgebraic dimension theory (Tarski–Seidenberg). ∎
Corollary (Width–depth tradeoff). For canonical degree-2 stacks with $w = O(1)$, the required depth is $L \ge p/(2w^2+3) = \Omega(p)$. Logarithmic depth suffices only if width grows with $p$.

The key structural message: Degree-2 attention is optimization-sufficient (same $d^2$ landscape) but simulation-insufficient (cannot universally replicate arbitrary polynomial attention in fixed width).

8. The $O(nd^3)$ Squared Attention Block

The unnormalized squared-attention numerator $Y_i = \sum_j (q_i^\top k_j)^2 v_j$ admits an exact factorization via a precomputed tensor:

$$T_{ab} = \sum_{j=1}^n k_{ja} k_{jb} v_j, \quad Y_i = \sum_{a,b=1}^d q_{ia} q_{ib} T_{ab},$$

giving $O(nd^2 d_v)$ complexity, i.e. $O(nd^3)$ when $d_v = d$.

Mechanism Per-head time Blocks needed Status
Softmax attention $O(n^2 d)$ $L$ Baseline
Positive polynomial (degree $p$) $O(n r_p d_v)$ $L$ Same-depth replacement (Thm 5)
Squared attention ($p=2$) $O(nd^3)$ $L + O(L\log p)$ Conditional depth compensation (Thm 6)

9. Experiments

9.1 Numerical verification of the theorems

  • Pullback theorem sanity check: relative Frobenius error $\sim 10^{-16}$ in double precision.
  • Generic rank theorem: exact agreement with $r(r-\varepsilon_X)$ prediction for all tested $(r, \varepsilon_X)$ combinations.
  • Same-depth replacement: Degree-24 positive polynomial preserves accuracy and logits ($< 10^{-3}$ max deviation) in kernel-only swap test.

10. Discussion

What our theorems prove

Theorem 1 identifies the exact optimization-relevant object (the sandwich transform). Theorem 2 shows the corresponding Jacobian rank is generically $d^2$. Theorem 3 proves same-depth softmax→polynomial replacement. Theorem 4 isolates the degree-2 question into a simulation hypothesis. Theorem 5 establishes an unconditional $\Omega(p)$ lower bound for fixed-width simulation.

What they do not prove

They do not show that depth alone solves training stability for polynomial attention. Stability requires suitable scaling and normalization. Our theorems are expressivity and optimization-dimension results; stability is a separate design constraint.

The central conceptual point

Attention should not be understood primarily as an $n \times n$ matrix operation. It should be understood as a bilinear $d^2$-dimensional problem viewed through an $n^2$-dimensional rendering.
